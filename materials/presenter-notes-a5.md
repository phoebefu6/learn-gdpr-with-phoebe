# Presenter notes - Leader session 5: Breach + penalties

**Session goal (one line).** Fix two numbers in every leader's head - 72 hours and 4% of global turnover - and make both concrete against the PDPA and the real fines.

## Run of show (45 min)

- 0-3 - Welcome. Open with the two numbers: 72 hours to tell the regulator from awareness, 4% of global turnover as the ceiling.

- 3-20 - Concepts. Part 1 (9 min): the 72-hour clock, the two thresholds (authority any risk, individuals high risk), and the PDPA contrast (~30-day assess, 3-day notify, 500 threshold). Part 2 (8 min): the two fine tiers (Art 83), global turnover, the Meta and Amazon cases, and why transfers dominate the top of the list (self-study).

- 20-40 - Exercises. Exercise 1 (12 min): run your own 72-hour clock on a Monday-9am breach. Exercise 2 (8 min): size the exposure - 4% of global vs 10% of Singapore turnover.

- 40-45 - Q&A and the five DPO/security questions.

## Preflight

- Open a5-breach-penalties.html, Expand all, test Projector zoom.

- Know your org's global vs Singapore turnover (or a plausible figure) so exercise 2 produces a real gap.

## Never-cut beats

- The clock starts when you become AWARE, not when the investigation is complete - the single most common planning mistake.

- Two thresholds: any risk goes to the authority within 72 hours; high risk also goes to the individuals (Art 34).

- The fine ceiling is a percentage of GLOBAL turnover, so for an internationally exposed company the GDPR tail-risk dwarfs the PDPA cap.

## Cuts if running long

- Drop the "why transfers cases dominate" self-study card (session a6 picks it up).

- Trim exercise 1 to just mapping the hops and adding them against 72 hours.

## Quiz answers

1. B - The clock starts when you become aware of the breach (Art 33), not when the investigation finishes.

2. C - EUR 20 million or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher (Art 83(5)).

3. A - Notify individuals when the breach is likely to result in a high risk to their rights (Art 34); the 500 threshold is a PDPA rule, not a GDPR one.

## Common questions + crisp answers

- "What if we cannot finish investigating in 72 hours?" You can notify in phases - file what you have and follow up; the clock does not wait for a complete picture.

- "Does every breach go to the regulator?" No - only breaches that risk people's rights. One unlikely to result in a risk needs no authority notice, but document that judgement.

- "Which is our bigger fine risk, a leak or a transfer?" As the Meta EUR 1.2bn case shows, cross-border transfers sit at the top of the list - session a6 is where that risk lives for a SG company.

## Compliance caution

Educational, not legal advice. Both the PDPC and EU authorities weigh harm and culpability case by case; present the ceilings as tail-risk, not as expected fines.
