# Presenter notes - Practitioner session 4: Handling a DSAR

**Session goal (one line).** Build a five-step DSAR workflow (intake, verify, locate, respond, package) that reliably delivers inside the one-month clock without exposing anyone else's data.

## Run of show (45 min)

- 0-3 - Welcome. Frame it: the access request is the right you handle most, and it arrives as an ordinary email that starts a one-month clock.

- 3-18 - Concepts (15 min). Part 1: what you must provide (Art 15), verify identity first without stalling, protect third parties, free by default; the one-month clock (Art 12(3)) and the handoff risk.

- 18-40 - Build-along (22 min). Part 2: when you can extend or refuse (manifestly unfounded or excessive), and one pipeline serving erasure/portability/objection (self-study). Then the "build Lumina's DSAR workflow" exercise.

- 40-45 - Q&A and Lumina-after-b4 recap.

## Preflight

- Open b4-dsar.html, Expand all, test Projector zoom.

- Have the DSAR-pipeline SVG and the clock/extension/refusal SVG ready.

- Know your org's real request path (inbox, owner, systems) so the workflow exercise is concrete.

## Never-cut beats

- One month from receipt (Art 12(3)), extendable by two more for complex or numerous requests, with notice inside the first month.

- Verify identity but do not use it as a stalling tactic; redact third-party personal data from the response.

- A named owner and a single intake channel is most of DSAR compliance - a request that waits in an inbox has already half-spent the clock.

## Cuts if running long

- Drop the "one pipeline, many rights" self-study card (mention it in one line).

- Trim the workflow exercise to intake and respond (set an internal three-week deadline).

## Quiz answers

1. B - One month, extendable by up to two more for complex or numerous requests (Art 12(3)).

2. C - Redact or withhold the other person's data and provide the rest; disclosing a third party's data would itself be a breach.

3. A - You may charge or refuse only when a request is manifestly unfounded or excessive - and you should document the decision.

## Common questions + crisp answers

- "Do we build a separate process for erasure and portability?" No - they ride the same pipeline as access; only the action at the "act" step changes (delete, export, stop).

- "Can we take longer if we are just busy?" No - "it is a hassle" is not the bar. Extensions are for genuinely complex or numerous requests, with notice and reasons in the first month.

- "What is a safe internal deadline?" Aim for three weeks, leaving buffer against the one-month legal limit, and name who redacts and who approves the send.

## Compliance caution

Educational, not legal advice. Get advice on edge cases, especially refusals - "manifestly unfounded or excessive" is a high bar, and a wrong refusal is a common, avoidable complaint.
