# Presenter notes - Leader session 2: 7 principles, 6 lawful bases

**Session goal (one line).** Leave leaders able to hold the seven Article 5 principles and six Article 6 lawful bases in their head, and to break the PDPA consent-first reflex.

## Run of show (45 min)

- 0-3 - Welcome. Frame it: session 1 settled whether GDPR applies; this answers what it makes you do, and it all sits on two short lists.

- 3-20 - Concepts. Part 1 (9 min): the six handling principles plus accountability (Art 5(2)), the one with teeth. Part 2 (9 min): the six co-equal bases, the real consent standard (Art 4(11), 7), and special-category data (Art 9) plus legitimate interests as self-study.

- 20-40 - Exercises. Exercise 1 (12 min): map your purposes to a basis, one line each, flag special-category. Exercise 2 (8 min): the "are we over-relying on consent?" audit, four questions.

- 40-45 - Q&A and the five DPO questions.

## Preflight

- Open a2-principles-bases.html, Expand all, test Projector zoom.

## Never-cut beats

- Accountability (Art 5(2)) reshapes the other six: it is not enough to comply, you must be able to demonstrate it - records, not good intentions.

- The six bases are co-equal doors, not a hierarchy with consent on top; for core operations contract or legitimate interests usually fits better.

- Special-category data (Art 9) needs an Art 9(2) condition ON TOP of an Art 6 basis, not instead of one.

## Cuts if running long

- Drop the "how the principles echo the PDPA" self-study card.

- Trim exercise 2 to the first and third questions (how many sit on consent; what breaks if withdrawn).

## Quiz answers

1. B - Accountability (Art 5(2)) requires being able to demonstrate compliance, not just comply; the burden sits with the organisation.

2. C - Consent is one of six co-equal lawful bases, often not the best fit for core operations because it can be withdrawn.

3. A - Special-category data needs an Article 9(2) condition on top of a normal Article 6 basis; two locks, not one.

## Common questions + crisp answers

- "If we already get consent, why change anything?" Consent evaporates when withdrawn. If the processing is necessary to run the service, contract or legitimate interests is a firmer basis that does not vanish.

- "What exactly is a balancing test for legitimate interests?" You document the interest, its necessity, and the balance against the person's rights - often called an LIA. Show your working; it is not a free pass.

- "Do we need consent for analytics?" Not necessarily - routine, privacy-respecting analytics can sit on legitimate interests with a documented LIA; reserve consent for genuine marketing.

## Compliance caution

Educational, not legal advice. Note that "LIA" (legitimate interests assessment) is the method name the ICO and EDPB use, not statutory wording in the Regulation - teach it as the recognised method, not a quoted legal term.
