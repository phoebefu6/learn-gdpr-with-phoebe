# Presenter notes - Leader session 3: Data-subject rights vs PDPA

**Session goal (one line).** Make leaders recognise the eight GDPR rights, the one-month clock, and exactly which four rights the PDPA does not give, so they know what a dual-regime operation must add.

## Run of show (45 min)

- 0-3 - Welcome. Frame it: scope decides if GDPR applies; rights decide what you owe people - and this is where the two regimes diverge most.

- 3-20 - Concepts. Part 1 (9 min): the eight rights in the order they get used, and the one-month clock (Art 12(3), extendable by two). Part 2 (8 min): what the PDPA does not give - the four rights that need new process - plus the Do Not Call marketing point as self-study.

- 20-40 - Exercises. Exercise 1 (12 min): audit your request-handling, mark the four GDPR-only rights yes/no, circle the biggest gap. Exercise 2 (8 min): set the one-month drill, trace your access-request clock.

- 40-45 - Q&A and the five DPO questions.

## Never-cut beats

- The clock: respond within one month (Art 12(3)), extendable by up to two more for complex or numerous requests, with notice inside the first month.

- The four rights the PDPA does not give: erasure, restriction, objection, automated-decision review (portability is legislated in the PDPA but not in force).

- Build to the GDPR's broader set once and it covers the PDPA automatically; build to the PDPA first and you are four rights short.

## Cuts if running long

- Drop the "where PDPA marketing rights actually live" self-study card.

- Trim exercise 2 to the first two steps (trace the path, add it up).

## Quiz answers

1. B - One month, extendable by up to two further months for complex or numerous requests (Art 12(3)).

2. C - Erasure (right to be forgotten); access and correction exist under both regimes, erasure does not exist under the PDPA.

3. A - Build to the GDPR's broader set of rights; it covers the PDPA's narrower set automatically.

## Common questions + crisp answers

- "Can we charge for an access request?" No, free by default; a reasonable fee or a refusal is only for manifestly unfounded or excessive requests.

- "Is erasure absolute - do we always have to delete?" No, it applies in defined circumstances (data no longer needed, consent withdrawn), but operationally it is the hard one because you must delete everywhere, including backups and downstream systems.

- "Do a GDPR objection and a PDPA marketing opt-out go to the same place?" They should feed one suppression list, not two - the GDPR objection to marketing and the PDPA Do Not Call path can be satisfied by a single "stop marketing me" signal.

## Compliance caution

Educational, not legal advice. Verify the current PDPA data-portability position - it is legislated (Part 6B) but not in force, so confirm before telling anyone it is or is not available.
