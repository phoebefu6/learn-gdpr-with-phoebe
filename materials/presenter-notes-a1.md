# Presenter notes - Leader session 1: Why GDPR reaches a Singapore company (Art 3)

**Session goal (one line).** Get every leader able to run the three-trigger Article 3 test on their own company and know whether they are single-regime (PDPA) or dual-regime (PDPA + GDPR).

## Run of show (45 min)

- 0-3 - Welcome. Open with the hook: "GDPR is not Europe's problem if you sell to or track people in the EU." Name the stakes early (4% of global turnover).

- 3-20 - Concepts. Part 1 (9 min): the three triggers (establishment, offering, monitoring) and the person-in-the-EU pivot; the stakes vs the PDPA; Art 27 representative as self-study, mention only. Part 2 (8 min): when both laws apply and the stricter-of-two table.

- 20-40 - Exercises. Exercise 1 (12 min): run the Article 3 test on their own business, write the evidence, circle single- vs dual-regime. Exercise 2 (8 min): the stricter-of-the-two audit, four questions.

- 40-45 - Q&A and the "five questions for your DPO" close.

## Never-cut beats

- Any ONE of the three triggers brings you into scope - no EU office needed for offering or monitoring.

- The test is where the PERSON is (in the EU), not their nationality and not where you sit.

- The fine base is GLOBAL turnover, so GDPR exposure dwarfs the PDPA's Singapore-turnover cap for a multinational.

## Cuts if running long

- Drop the Art 27 EU representative self-study card first (it is flagged self-study anyway).

- Shorten exercise 2 to two questions (rights + transfers) and set the rest as homework.

## Quiz answers

1. C - Offering goods or services to people in the EU (Art 3(2)(a)) is a trigger; no EU office and payment not required.

2. A - EUR 20 million or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher; the global base is the point.

3. B - Erasure (right to be forgotten) is a GDPR right with no live PDPA equivalent; access and correction exist under both.

## Common questions + crisp answers

- "A French tourist buys from us in Singapore - are we caught?" Different question. The trigger turns on the individual being physically in the EU, not their nationality; a one-off in-store sale is not targeting people in the EU.

- "Our website is just accessible from Europe - is that enough?" No. Offering turns on evidence of targeting (EU shipping, euro pricing, EU-language marketing, actual EU customers), not mere accessibility.

- "If we are in scope, what do we do first?" Appoint an EU representative (Art 27) and confirm a lawful basis for the EU data you already hold; the practitioner track walks the full list.

## Compliance caution

Educational, not legal advice. Flag the PDPA nuance carefully: the PDPA can reach overseas orgs, but through data activity connected to Singapore, not a GDPR-style "targeting or monitoring" test - there is no clean PDPA parallel to Art 3(2), so do not overstate the symmetry.
