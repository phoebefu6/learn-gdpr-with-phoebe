# Presenter notes - Leader session 4: Controller, processor, DPO, DPIA

**Session goal (one line).** Get leaders labelling their own company and every vendor as controller or processor per data flow, and knowing the narrow cases where a DPO or a DPIA is actually mandatory.

## Run of show (45 min)

- 0-3 - Welcome. Frame it: almost every GDPR duty is addressed to "the controller", so the first question in any data relationship is who decides.

- 3-20 - Concepts. Part 1 (9 min): the controller-vs-processor test (who decides purposes and means) and the Article 28 DPA terms; "you are usually the controller for your customers" as self-study. Part 2 (8 min): when a DPO is mandatory (Art 37, three cases) with the PDPA "DPO for every org" contrast, and when a DPIA is required (Art 35, three examples).

- 20-40 - Exercises. Exercise 1 (12 min): map Lumina's (or your own) vendors and mark every Art 28 DPA. Exercise 2 (8 min): run the DPO and DPIA tests honestly.

- 40-45 - Q&A and the five DPO/counsel questions.

## Preflight

- Open a4-controller-processor.html, Expand all, test Projector zoom.

- Have the controller-processor-DPA SVG and the DPO/DPIA triggers SVG ready.

- Bring a real vendor list (cloud host, email, analytics, payment, support, payroll) to make exercise 1 fast.

## Never-cut beats

- Whoever decides the WHY and the HOW is the controller - size and seniority are irrelevant; even a giant cloud vendor is your processor for your customer data.

- Every controller-to-processor flow needs a binding Article 28 DPA (instructions-only, confidentiality, Art 32 security, sub-processors, assist, delete/return, audit).

- DPO is mandatory only in three GDPR cases - but the PDPA requires one for every org, so most SG firms already have one.

## Cuts if running long

- Drop the "you are usually the controller" self-study card.

- Trim exercise 2 to the DPO test only and set the DPIA test as homework.

## Quiz answers

1. B - Your company is the controller; you decided the purpose and means, the cloud host is your processor regardless of its size (Art 4(7)).

2. C - A DPO is mandatory only in defined cases (public authority, or core-activity large-scale monitoring or large-scale special/criminal data); "every org always" is the PDPA rule.

3. A - A DPIA is required when processing is likely to result in a high risk - the three examples (extensive profiling with effects, large-scale sensitive data, large-scale public monitoring).

## Common questions + crisp answers

- "Can one company be both controller and processor?" Yes, for different data sets - your cloud vendor is your processor for customer data but a controller for its own billing relationship with you. Label per data flow, not per company.

- "Do we need a DPIA for everything?" No, only for high-risk processing. The three statutory examples are the floor; authorities also publish their own must-DPIA lists.

- "We have a DPO already for the PDPA - is that enough?" The role can serve both, but check independence and reporting line are real, not nominal.

## Compliance caution

Educational, not legal advice. "Large-scale", "core activity", and "high risk" are judgement calls, not bright lines - treat the three DPIA examples as the floor and confirm close calls against your authority's guidance.
