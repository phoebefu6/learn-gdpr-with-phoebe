Part 0

The clock starts before you are ready

A breach never arrives at a convenient time, and the GDPR does not care. The 72-hour clock (Article 33) starts when you become aware a breach has occurred - not when you finish investigating, not on Monday morning. That single fact reshapes incident response: you cannot afford a plan where the first hours are lost to "who owns this?". This session makes the response concrete and, crucially, timed.

Part 1 · covers Articles 33 & 34

Assess, then notify 7 min live

Two thresholds decide who you tell. Any breach that risks people's rights goes to the authority within 72 hours. A breach that risks them badly also goes to the affected individuals.

LiveThe notifiable test + the two clocks3 min▶
  • To the authority (Art 33): notify without undue delay and, where feasible, within 72 hours of becoming aware - unless the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to people's rights. Late means you must explain the delay.
  • To individuals (Art 34): when the breach is likely to result in a high risk to them, tell them too, without undue delay. Exceptions include data that was strongly encrypted (a payoff for session b6's measures).
  • If you are a processor: a processor that detects a breach must notify the controller it works for, without undue delay - you are a link in someone else's 72 hours.

Livevs the PDPA clock, for a dual-regime breach3 min▶

A breach at a Singapore company with EU customers triggers both regimes, and the clocks differ:

  • GDPR: one 72-hour clock from awareness, risk-based, no headcount threshold for the authority.
  • PDPA: assess (the PDPC expects the cycle within about 30 days), then notify the PDPC within 3 calendar days of determining it notifiable, gated by significant harm or 500+ individuals.
  • The binding constraint is the GDPR. Build your response to hit 72 hours and you comfortably meet the PDPA timeline on the same incident.
Real world

Lumina's exposed backup. Lumina discovers a misconfigured backup exposed 3,000 records - EU and Singapore. It becomes aware Friday 4pm. The GDPR clock is now running through the weekend. Because Lumina had a named incident owner with authority to notify, it contained the exposure, assessed the risk, and filed with the EU authority within 72 hours - and met the PDPA process on the same facts. The encryption from b6 meant individual notice was not required for the encrypted portion.

Part 2 · the response, run well

Contain, notify, learn 8 min live

A good breach response is not just fast notification; it is a loop that ends with the gap closed. What you notify, and what you fix, both matter.

LiveWhat to include, and when to consult first3 min▶
  • The notification tells the authority: the nature of the breach, the categories and rough number of individuals and records affected, the likely consequences, and the measures taken or proposed. You can provide information in phases if you do not have it all in 72 hours.
  • The individual notice (when required) is in clear language: what happened, likely consequences, what you are doing, and what they can do (change a password, watch for fraud).
  • Consult the authority first for breaches likely to attract widespread attention - it is better to be seen managing it than reacting.
  • Close the loop: the incident should upgrade a control (the Protection Obligation gap from the leader track) so the same breach cannot recur.
Self-studyThe response plan is the deliverable, not the response2 min read▶

You cannot write a good breach response in the 72 hours you have to deliver one - the plan has to exist beforehand. The real work of this session is not "know the rules"; it is "have a plan, with named roles, that you have timed". A plan names who becomes the incident owner, who can authorise a regulator notification without waiting for a committee, how the security team escalates to them out of hours, and where the notification templates live. Then you rehearse it - a tabletop exercise where you start the clock on a Friday afternoon and see whether you would actually make 72 hours. Most teams discover their bottleneck is not the technical investigation but the approval step: nobody is sure they are allowed to notify. Fixing that - a single owner with pre-granted authority - is worth more than any amount of policy text. Build the plan, time it, and keep the templates ready.

Check yourself

Three questions before you go 🎯 ◐ 90 seconds

1 · The 72-hour breach clock starts...

Article 33: notify within 72 hours of becoming aware. The clock runs through weekends and does not wait for the investigation to conclude - which is why a pre-agreed owner matters.

2 · You must notify affected individuals (not just the authority) when the breach...

Article 34: individuals are told when the risk to them is high. Strong encryption of the affected data can remove that duty - the payoff of good supplementary measures.

3 · The most common real-world bottleneck in hitting 72 hours is...

Teams usually find the technical work is manageable; the delay is organisational. A single incident owner with pre-granted authority to notify is the fix.
