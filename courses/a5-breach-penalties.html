Breach and penalties

Two numbers define GDPR risk for a leader: 72 hours and 4%. Seventy-two hours is how long you have to tell a regulator after you become aware of a breach. Four percent - of global turnover - is the ceiling on the fine if you get the serious things wrong. This session makes both concrete, sets them against the PDPA's own regime, and shows the billion-euro cases that prove the numbers are real.

Part 0

The two numbers that focus the mind

Most of the GDPR is about doing things well in advance. Breach and penalties are about what happens when you did not - and they are what get a board's attention. The 72-hour clock is unforgiving because it starts when you become aware, not when you have finished investigating. And the fines are tied to global turnover, so for any company with international revenue the GDPR ceiling dwarfs the PDPA's. This hour turns both into something you can plan for.

Part 1 · covers Articles 33 and 34

The 72-hour clock 9 min live

The breach rules have two thresholds and one deadline. Any breach that risks people's rights goes to the regulator within 72 hours. A breach that risks them badly also goes to the individuals.

LiveTwo thresholds, one deadline3 min▶
  • To the authority (Art 33): without undue delay and, where feasible, no later than 72 hours after becoming aware - unless the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to people's rights. Miss 72 hours and you must explain the delay.
  • To the individuals (Art 34): when the breach is likely to result in a high risk to them, tell them too, without undue delay. Exceptions exist - notably if the data was strongly encrypted.
  • The trap is "aware". The clock does not wait for your investigation. The moment you have reasonable certainty a breach occurred, it is running.
★ Try it now (any chat AI)Explain the GDPR breach-notification rules to an executive: the 72-hour clock, what "become aware" means, and the difference between notifying the regulator (any risk) and notifying affected individuals (high risk). Then contrast it with Singapore's PDPA breach timeline.
Livevs the PDPA clock3 min▶

The PDPA runs a different clock. You assess whether a breach is notifiable (the PDPC expects the whole cycle within about 30 days), and once you determine it is, you notify the PDPC within 3 calendar days. The threshold is significant harm or 500 or more affected individuals.

  • GDPR: one 72-hour clock from awareness, risk-based, no numeric headcount threshold for the authority.
  • PDPA: a ~30-day assessment window, then 3 days from determining notifiable, gated by the 500/significant-harm threshold.
  • For a dual-regime company: the GDPR clock is tighter and starts earlier. Build your incident response to the 72-hour standard and you comfortably meet the PDPA one too.
Real world

Lumina's Monday incident. Lumina discovers an exposed database on a Monday morning affecting EU and Singapore users. Two clocks start. The GDPR one is the binding constraint: 72 hours to notify the EU authority. The team that had only planned for the PDPA's 30-day assessment window would already be late.

Part 2 · covers Article 83

Two tiers, global turnover 8 min live

GDPR fines come in two tiers, and the ceiling is a percentage of worldwide turnover. That single word is why GDPR exposure is a different order of magnitude from the PDPA.

LiveThe two tiers3 min▶
TierCeiling (whichever is higher)Covers
Lower (Art 83(4))EUR 10 million or 2% of global annual turnoverRecords, security, breach notification, DPIA, DPO duties (Art 25-39)
Upper (Art 83(5))EUR 20 million or 4% of global annual turnoverPrinciples, lawful basis, consent (Art 5/6/7/9); rights (Art 12-22); transfers (Art 44-49); ignoring an order
  • "Whichever is higher" means a large company is measured against the percentage, not the euro figure - the cap scales with revenue.
  • Global turnover is the base, not EU turnover. A multinational's ceiling is enormous.
LiveThe cases that prove it - and the PDPA contrast3 min▶
  • Meta - EUR 1.2 billion (2023): the largest GDPR fine to date, for unlawful transfers of EU user data to the US. A transfers case (upper tier).
  • Amazon - EUR 746 million (2021): over its advertising and data-processing practices.
  • The PDPA contrast: Singapore caps at S$1 million or 10% of Singapore turnover. For a company with modest Singapore revenue but large global revenue, the GDPR exposure is vastly larger, because the base is global.
The leadership read The PDPC and EU authorities both weigh harm and culpability, and both prefer directions to fines where they can. But the ceilings tell you where the tail risk lives: for an internationally exposed company, that is the GDPR, tied to global turnover.
Self-studyWhy the transfers cases dominate the top of the list2 min read▶

It is not a coincidence that the biggest fine on record is a transfers case. International data transfer (session a6) is where a Singapore company is most exposed, because moving EU personal data to a Singapore server is exactly the kind of transfer the GDPR scrutinises after Schrems II. Meta's EUR 1.2 billion was not for a leak or a consent slip - it was for sending EU data abroad without adequate protection. For a Singapore business, that is the risk that maps most directly onto your own operations: your servers, your cloud region, your EU customers' data crossing a border. It is why the leader track ends on transfers, and why the practitioner track dedicates a full session to doing them right.

Exercise 1 of 2

Run your 72-hour clock ★ 12 min · pen and paper

A breach plan that has never been timed is a hope, not a plan.

Imagine a breach discovered at 9am Monday affecting EU individuals. Write who first "becomes aware" in your organisation, and how the alarm reaches a decision-maker.

Map the hops to a regulator notification: who assesses risk, who drafts, who approves, who submits. Put an hour estimate on each.

Add it up against 72 hours. If a weekend or an approval bottleneck eats the budget, you have found a real gap.

Decide the one change that would most protect the clock - usually a named incident owner with authority to notify.

Exercise 2 of 2

Size the exposure ★ 8 min · your next board prep

Put the two regimes side by side on your own numbers.

Take your global annual turnover. Compute 4% of it - that is your GDPR upper-tier ceiling if it exceeds EUR 20 million.

Take your Singapore turnover. Compute 10% - the PDPA ceiling (or S$1 million if larger applies).

Compare. For most internationally-exposed companies the GDPR number is far larger. Note the gap.

Ask: does our board understand that our largest privacy tail-risk is GDPR, not PDPA? If not, that is the slide to add.

Why size it "GDPR is serious" loses to other priorities. "Our GDPR ceiling is X, our PDPA ceiling is Y, and X is ten times Y because it is on global revenue" reframes privacy as an enterprise risk.

Check yourself

Three questions before you go 🎯 ◐ 90 seconds

1 · The GDPR 72-hour breach clock starts when...

Article 33: notify without undue delay and, where feasible, within 72 hours of becoming aware. Awareness starts the clock - not the end of the investigation.

2 · The GDPR upper-tier fine ceiling is...

Article 83(5): EUR 20M or 4% of global turnover, whichever is higher - covering principles, bases, rights, and transfers. The global base is why it dwarfs the PDPA cap.

3 · You must notify affected INDIVIDUALS (not just the authority) when the breach...

Article 34: individuals are notified when the risk to them is high. (The 500 threshold is a PDPA rule, not a GDPR one.) Any risk goes to the authority; high risk also goes to individuals.
