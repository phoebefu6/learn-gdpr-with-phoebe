Part 0

The task behind the biggest fine

Moving EU personal data to a server outside the EU is the most scrutinised thing a non-EU company does under the GDPR. Singapore has no adequacy decision, so every EU-to-Singapore flow needs a mechanism - and, since Schrems II, more than a signed contract. This session turns that into a concrete, repeatable procedure: SCCs, a transfer impact assessment, and supplementary measures. Get it right once and it protects every EU data flow you run.

Part 1 · covers Article 46 + Schrems II

SCCs, then the assessment 7 min live

The modern transfer is three moves: sign the Standard Contractual Clauses, assess the destination country, and add measures where the assessment shows a gap. Skipping the middle move is exactly what Schrems II forbids.

LivePutting SCCs in place3 min▶
  • What SCCs are: a pre-approved contract, published by the European Commission, between the data exporter (in the EU) and the importer (you, in Singapore). You use the correct module for your relationship (controller-to-processor, etc.) and fill in the annexes describing the data and measures.
  • Who signs: for a Singapore company receiving EU customer data, your EU customers (the exporters) and you (the importer). Many put the SCCs inside the data processing agreement (session a4's Art 28 contract).
  • Not set-and-forget: the annexes must actually describe your real processing and measures, and you revisit them when things change.

LiveThe transfer impact assessment3 min▶

The TIA is the Schrems II homework: before you rely on SCCs, you assess whether the destination country's law would undermine the protection the SCCs promise - chiefly, can its government access the data in ways an EU person could not challenge?

  • What it asks: what data is transferred, to whom, under what local laws, and whether those laws permit disproportionate government access without redress.
  • Then, measures: where the assessment finds a gap, add supplementary measures. The strongest are technical - strong encryption with keys held outside the destination, pseudonymisation - because they protect the data even if local law reaches the server.
  • Document it: the TIA is your evidence that you did the Schrems II analysis, not just signed a template.
Real world

Lumina's Singapore region. Lumina hosts EU customer data in a Singapore cloud region. It puts SCCs into its customer DPA, runs a TIA on Singapore, and encrypts the data at rest with keys held in an EU-based key service it controls - so even if the data sat on the SG disk, it is unreadable without keys outside SG. That combination is what makes the transfer defensible.

Part 2 · the flows you forget

Sub-processors and the data map 8 min live

The transfer you sign SCCs for is the obvious one. The transfers that catch people are the ones hidden in the tools: analytics, support, email, backups - each may move EU data somewhere, and each is your responsibility.

LiveCover the sub-processors3 min▶
  • Every vendor that processes EU data is a transfer if it sits outside the EU. Your analytics tool, your help desk, your email sender, your cloud backups.
  • The chain flows down: your processor's sub-processors matter too. Your DPA (Art 28) should require SCCs down the chain, and your ROPA's transfers column (session b5) is where you track them.
  • The audit question: for every row in your transfers column, is there a named mechanism - SCCs, adequacy, or a derogation? A blank is an unlawful transfer waiting to be found.
The reuse This is why the ROPA came first. The transfers column you built in b5 is the exact list you now attach mechanisms to. Compliance here is not a fresh project - it is finishing the inventory.
Self-studyWhy data localisation is sometimes the simplest answer2 min read▶

Sometimes the cleanest way to solve a transfer problem is to not transfer. If your EU customers' data can live in an EU cloud region, then for that data there is no third-country transfer at all - no SCCs, no TIA, no supplementary measures needed for it. Many cloud providers make this a configuration choice: pick an EU region for EU customers. For a Singapore company, this is worth weighing against the operational cost of running EU infrastructure. The trade is real: localisation removes the hardest compliance task entirely for the localised data, at the price of some architectural complexity and cost. It is not always right - your analytics and support tools may still transfer - but for the core customer database, "keep EU data in the EU" can be simpler and safer than perfecting an SCCs-plus-TIA-plus-encryption stack. Treat localisation as a genuine option, not a last resort.

Build-along · take it further

Lawful transfers for Lumina ★ 10 min · pen and paper

Attach a mechanism to every EU-out flow.

Take your transfers column from the b5 ROPA (or Lumina's: SG servers, analytics, support, backups). List each destination.

Name the mechanism for each: adequacy (rare), SCCs, or a derogation. Flag blanks.

For the Singapore server flow, write the three-step plan: SCCs in the DPA, a TIA on Singapore, encryption with keys held outside SG.

Consider localisation for the core customer DB: would an EU region remove the transfer entirely? Note the trade-off.

Check yourself

Three questions before you go 🎯 ◐ 90 seconds

1 · For a Singapore company receiving EU data, the modern lawful transfer is...

Post-Schrems II, SCCs alone are not enough. You must assess the destination (a TIA) and add measures - especially strong encryption - where local law falls short.

2 · The strongest supplementary measure for a transfer to Singapore is usually...

Technical measures carry the most weight. Encryption with keys you hold outside Singapore protects the data even if local law could reach the server.

3 · Your US analytics vendor processes EU user data. This is...

Every sub-processor outside the EU that touches EU data is a transfer you own. Track it in your ROPA transfers column and ensure SCCs flow down the chain.
